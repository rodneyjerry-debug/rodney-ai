AI Governance Framework for Financial Services

A practical, regulator-aware blueprint for governing AI across a financial institution. Designed to extend — not replace — your existing model risk, operational risk and three-lines-of-defense disciplines.

The five pillars

Effective AI governance in FS rests on five mutually reinforcing pillars. Weakness in any one creates exposure that the others cannot fully compensate for.

1. Policy & Standards

An enterprise AI policy approved by the board, supplemented by standards for model development, GenAI usage, third-party AI and data use.

2. Decision Rights & Committees

Clear RACI for AI decisions. Most institutions need an AI Steering Committee (executive) and an AI Risk Committee (independent), feeding into existing risk governance.

3. Risk Identification & Controls

An AI risk taxonomy mapped to existing operational, model and conduct risk frameworks, with controls embedded in the SDLC and MLOps pipeline.

4. Inventory & Lifecycle

A single AI/ML inventory that tracks every model from intake through decommissioning, with risk tiering, validation status and ownership.

5. Reporting & Assurance

Dashboards for the board, regulators and external auditors that prove AI is being used responsibly — not just claimed to be.

6. People & Culture

AI literacy at every level, escalation paths people actually use, and incentives that reward raising concerns rather than hiding them.

Policy template outline

A board-approved AI policy should cover these elements. Use this outline as a template for your own enterprise policy.

  1. Purpose & scope. States the institution's commitment to responsible AI, and defines which systems are in and out of scope (e.g., GenAI, third-party, traditional ML).
  2. Principles. Five to seven core principles (e.g., fairness, explainability, human oversight, compliance, accountability) with concise definitions.
  3. Governance structure. Roles, committees, escalation paths. Define AI Steering Committee (executive decision-making) and AI Risk Committee (independent oversight) charters.
  4. Risk management. Reference to risk appetite statement, risk tiering model, and core controls (intake gate, validation, monitoring, decommissioning).
  5. Model lifecycle. Intake through development, validation, deployment, monitoring and decommissioning. Point to the MRM checklist.
  6. GenAI-specific obligations. Foundation model selection, prompt governance, grounding/RAG architecture, hallucination controls, usage logging.
  7. Data governance. Data sourcing, quality requirements, PII/sensitive data handling, bias testing, representativeness.
  8. Third-party & vendor risk. Vendor assessment criteria, contractual controls (data residency, audit rights, liability), indemnity requirements.
  9. Regulatory compliance. Mapping to SR 11-7, SS1/23, EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF, GDPR, fair lending regs. Quarterly reporting cadence to 2nd line and board.
  10. Escalation & incident response. When and how to report AI failures, bias findings, compliance gaps. Who owns incident response.
  11. Training & literacy. Mandatory training for executives, tech leads, risk staff. Annual refresher cadence.
  12. Review & approval. Board sign-off, 2nd line ownership, annual refresh cycle.

Committee terms of reference (ToR) template

Two key committees manage AI governance. Here is a template structure for each.

AI Steering Committee

  • Purpose: Executive-level decision-making on AI strategy, portfolio prioritization, use case approvals (Tier 1 & 2), and major policy changes.
  • Membership: CIO/CTO, Chief Risk Officer, Chief Compliance Officer, Head of Model Risk Management, LOB heads (Investment Banking, Retail, Wealth, Ops), Chief Data Officer. Optional: Chief Innovation Officer, General Counsel.
  • Frequency: Monthly (minimum); more often if incidents or major regulatory changes.
  • Accountability: Accountable to the Board Risk Committee for AI strategy execution and risk appetite compliance.
  • Key decisions: Approve Tier 1 use cases; approve new GenAI pilots; review AI incident reports; approve AI vendor contracts above threshold; review regulatory feedback.
  • Escalation: Material breaches of risk appetite, regulatory criticism, or major incidents escalate to Board Risk Committee within 48 hours.

AI Risk Committee (Independent Oversight)

  • Purpose: Independent review of AI risk register, control effectiveness, regulatory alignment, and validation outcomes. Reports to the Board Audit & Risk Committees.
  • Membership: Chief Risk Officer (chair), independent 2nd line model risk head, external AI risk advisor (optional), Chief Compliance Officer, Head of Internal Audit (observer).
  • Frequency: Quarterly minimum; monthly if under active regulatory scrutiny.
  • Accountability: Reports findings and recommendations to the Board Risk Committee and Audit Committee.
  • Key reviews: Validation exception tracking; fairness testing results; monitoring alert trends; third-party vendor assessments; regulatory gap closure status.
  • Escalation: Unresolved control gaps, validation failures, or fairness breaches are escalated to the Board within 10 business days.

Implementation roadmap

Rolling out a governance framework is a 9–18 month program. This roadmap is a typical delivery sequence:

Phase 1: Foundation (Months 1–3)

  • Board workshop: AI strategy & risk appetite session. Agree on scope, risk tiers, committee structure.
  • Draft enterprise AI policy aligned to SR 11-7, EU AI Act, NIST RMF. Regulatory mapping workshop with compliance & risk teams.
  • Establish AI Steering & Risk Committees. Publish ToRs. Schedule first meetings.
  • Build AI inventory intake template. Begin data collection from all LOBs and tech teams.

Phase 2: Inventory & Classification (Months 3–6)

  • Complete AI inventory. Risk tier all models. Identify Tier 1 & 2 gaps in validation, monitoring.
  • Classify under EU AI Act, GDPR, fair lending regs. Identify high-risk systems requiring conformity.
  • Launch independent model risk function if not in place. Define validation scope & exception tracking.
  • Board presentation: Summary of AI portfolio, top risks, near-term remediation plan.

Phase 3: Controls & Validation (Months 6–12)

  • Deploy monitoring infrastructure for Tier 1 & 2 models. Define SLOs, alerting thresholds, escalation paths.
  • Fairness testing framework & tooling. Begin testing backlog of existing models.
  • GenAI governance: Establish usage policy, foundation model selection framework, prompt governance process. Deploy enterprise GenAI gateway if not existing.
  • Third-party vendor assessment. Build questionnaires, audit schedules, contract amendments.
  • AI literacy training for executives, tech leads, risk staff. Board & Risk Committee certification.

Phase 4: Assurance & Regulatory Readiness (Months 12–18)

  • Regulatory compliance gap closure. EU AI Act conformity documentation for high-risk systems.
  • Board dashboard launch. Quarterly AI risk reporting to the board begins.
  • External audit of AI governance. SOC 2 / Type II assessment if third-party AI dependencies material.
  • Regulator readiness. Prepare documentation for SR 11-7 exams, EU AI Act enforcement, SAMA inspections.
  • Establish AI incident & response playbook. Run tabletop exercises with crisis comms.

Quick reference: Common failure modes

Watch out for these patterns in AI governance programs:

  • Governance without teeth. Policies and committees that exist on paper but don't actually block bad models or slow down deployment. Fix: Tie governance decisions to promotion gates & budget allocation.
  • Shadow AI running rampant. Business teams using GenAI and external AI tools outside central visibility. Fix: Enforce firewall rules, deploy gateway, monitor SaaS app spending, offer approved alternatives.
  • Validation bottleneck. Independent validation function is undersourced and creates 6-month backlogs. Fix: Hire or contract validation expertise; deploy self-service validation tooling; risk-tier to avoid over-validating low-risk experiments.
  • Fairness testing theater. Fairness tests run but findings are not acted on; disparate impact is documented but unresolved. Fix: Make fairness test outcome a go/no-go gate; tie LOB incentives to fairness SLOs.
  • Stale inventory. AI inventory is built once and never maintained. Models drop off; new uses aren't registered. Fix: Make inventory a live operational system with quarterly audits; incentivize LOBs to keep it current.
  • Committee theater. Committees meet but don't make decisions; escalations are vague; accountability is diffuse. Fix: Publish meeting minutes with explicit decisions & owners; track action items with deadlines.
